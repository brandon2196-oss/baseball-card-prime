02Why this exists

This started as a dad-and-son rabbit hole. Cooper had the baseball card obsession. I had the itch to understand algorithms by building something real instead of just reading about them. A rare card gave the project a reason to exist: not hype, not resale value, not internet confetti — just a weird, durable little artifact we could verify ourselves.

The first time the digits snapped back into the card and the hash matched, the project stopped being a stunt and became the point: math, baseball, preservation, and learning all in the same sentence.

The Athletics rookie angle stayed on purpose — a small tip of the cap to Moneyball, and to the idea that baseball and math have always belonged together.

03How a number becomes a card

A digital image is a fixed list of RGB bytes. Read those bytes in order and you get one enormous integer. That integer usually is not prime, so the search moves upward until it reaches a prime. The published offset records exactly how far it moved.

①

Pixels become bytes

Every red, green, and blue value from the card image is read in a fixed order.

②

Bytes become an integer

The byte stream is interpreted as one giant number. Nothing mystical. Just a brutally large integer.

③

The integer becomes prime

The script searches upward, records the offset, and publishes the prime plus the metadata needed to reverse it.

04Why it is not an NFT

People ask because the phrase sounds collectible. But this does not need a chain, token, wallet, or marketplace. An NFT usually points to an asset somewhere else. This project is more old-school and more interesting: the number itself carries the image bytes.

The idea sits in the tradition of primes that contain other information — code, images, symbols — but this version is personal: a full-color sports-card artifact built to be decoded, checked, and understood without trusting me.

05How to check it yourself

The decoder above runs in your browser. It fetches the public prime and metadata, subtracts the offset, rebuilds the RGB pixels, and recomputes the SHA-256 hash against the recorded original image bytes. The repository has the primes, metadata, and scripts for anyone who wants to inspect the process instead of taking the page's word for it.

One honest distinction: this page proves the reconstruction — the published number minus its offset rebuilds the exact card image, confirmed by SHA-256 in your browser. It does not prove the number is prime; that's a separate claim, re-checked by the repository's verify.py (BPSW + Miller–Rabin). Related, but not the same thing.
